# A Very Brief Overview of Ethical Theory

Welcome back to A Little Bit of Philosophy. This is Philosophy 101, Unit 4, Lecture 1: An Overview of Ethics. 

We should start by putting ethics into the larger context of the various subjects of philosophy. Ethics is a sub-branch of axiology, which is one of the five main sub-disciplines of academic philosophy. Axiology is the philosophical investigation of value judgments, and a value judgment is a claim that one thing is better, or more desirable, or preferable to another. And we make many different kinds of discriminatory claims about the world around us: we might say that something is good, or beautiful, or just. 

And the various sub-branches of axiology each focus on one kind of value claim. Aesthetics for example, is the discipline that gives an analysis of the concept of beauty. I might say, for example, that the music of Pink Floyd is better than the music of Britney Spears. But in philosophy, we're not allowed to just make assertions; we're required to provide rational justification for such claims. So what makes one artist better than another? What makes one painting more beautiful than another? What makes one novel superior to another? These are the kinds of questions addressed in the field of aesthetics, which of course will break down into further sub-sub-disciplines like philosophy of architecture, philosophy of literature, philosophy of music, etc. 

Political theory is another subfield of axiology, and it focuses on the nature of justice: why is one form of social organization better than another, and what exactly does justice mean? Can we achieve justice, and if so, how do we achieve it? 

Ethics is yet another branch of axiology, and it will be the focus of this final set of videos for Philosophy 101. 

## The Focus of Ethical Theory

Now, before we go any further, we should note that moralism and ethics are not the same thing, even though they're obviously related. A moralist is someone who is committed to a particular theory of how to behave in the world, and their goal is to convince others (either through reason or rhetoric) to behave in that particular way. An ethicist, on the other hand, is someone who is committed to the philosophical analysis of morality and all of the various aspects relevant to moral thinking and action. That's not to say that an ethicist may not be committed to a particular moral theory; they usually are. But their interest is more broadly construed than simply imploring people to act or refrain from acting in certain ways. 

That leads us to the general questions and concerns (the areas of focus, if you will) of ethical theory. 

First, ethics investigates the nature of goodness itself, and how goodness might be achieved. After all, if we command someone to be good or do good, we can't hope to have met success if we don't know what the good is. If we don't know what it is we're commanding them to do, or how to be, so it would seem that an analysis of morality must include a thorough analysis of the nature of goodness itself before we attempt to achieve it. 

This of course leads to a second major question: how do we know the good? This is the epistemological component of ethics. If the good is a natural feature of the world, can we know it through empirical observation, or is it perhaps a non-natural feature of the world (like a Platonic universal, for example)? If so, is knowledge of the good innate to the mind of all rational agents? Or is it the case that the good is nothing more than a set of cultural norms and standards particular to the historical and cultural circumstances one finds themself living in? If that were true, knowing what's good would require nothing more than a sociological survey of those living in a particular culture. 

A third interesting question addressed in ethical theory regards the nature of moral language. We can think of this as the semantic dimension of ethical theory. When we say that an action is "right" or "wrong", "good" or "bad", what do these words actually mean? In fact, do they have meaning at all, or are they simply performative utterances (like a salutation or a greeting when you see another person) but not really full of content or meaning? 

As we try to answer these three fairly simple (or at least seemingly simple) questions, it's going to turn out that we're going to unlock a whole host of other questions that also need to be addressed. And this of course is going to lead to the field of ethics being a very broad field. 

So we're going to have to spend some time unpacking the many different dimensions of ethical theory. But in this video, we don't really have time to look at all of these various aspects of ethical theory. Our goal is simply to outline some of the main concerns of ethics as part of academic philosophy, and to provide a framework in which we can begin to address those basic questions. 

## The Subdivisions of Ethics: Normative Ethics and Metaethics

We'll begin with some of the basic vocabulary that will allow us to put our investigation of morality into sharper focus, and we should begin by noting the division of ethical theory into two main subdivisions, which we call normative ethics and metaethics. 

### Normative Ethics

Let's begin with normative ethics: the attempt to provide a systematic and rational justification of our moral intuitions. Look, none of us are born philosophers. We're born into a world that makes moral judgments, whether it's our family, or our society, or culture, or historical era, we find ourselves surrounded by others who are already making judgments about what's right and wrong, what's good and bad. And that's before we ever become aware of the fact that there may well be disagreement about precisely what is good or bad, right or wrong. 

But if we begin to look at the world through the glasses of the philosopher, we recognize that there is disagreement about what we should and shouldn't do, and more importantly, why we should or shouldn't do it, and how we should live in general. So clearly not all of these competing views can be correct. 

Normative ethics is the branch of ethical theory that investigates these differing or competing moral views. More simply, it's the part that investigates the competing theories that are trying to tell us what to do. That's exactly what the word "normative" means: action-guiding or action-controlling. As it turns out, there are a great many competing moral theories (different systems) all trying to tell us how we should govern our actions. Fortunately, these various competing theories can be organized under some convenient headings which will make them easier to analyze: 

1. **Teleological / Consequentialist Theories**: Teleological theories, also known as consequentialist theories, are a family of moral theories that all claim that moral obligation is determined by the consequences of our actions. What makes something right or wrong is precisely the consequences of the actions themselves. Of course, what counts as a good outcome is a matter of debate, which is why there's more than one teleological theory of morality, as we'll see later. 

2. **Deontological Theories**: A second group of moral theories claims that goodness is to be found not in the consequences of our actions (the ends never justify the means, in other words), but rather in the actions themselves, or perhaps in the motives of our actions. 

3. **Virtue Theories**: A third family of normative theories is called the virtue or aretaic theories (or virtue morality). Virtue theories place far less importance on our duties to act or not act in certain ways, and invites instead moral education and development. A virtue theory would hold that a good person does good things, a bad person does bad things, so the goal ought to be to create good people. That is, the goal of such a theory is to explain and help us how to become a virtuous person. So the development of moral character in an individual takes precedence over the articulation of any set of moral rules or guidelines that we ought to follow. 

### Metaethics

The second major division of ethical theory has nothing whatsoever to do with moral guidance. Metaethics is the branch of ethics that gives a meta-analysis of the various normative theories. 

1. **Descriptivism**: Descriptivism is the part of metaethics that investigates the objectivity or perhaps relativity of moral codes. Do we discover morality, or do we invent it? 
2. **Moral Semantics**: Moral semantics, on the other hand, is a more recent addition to ethical theory, rising to prominence in the 20th century. This branch of metaethics focuses on the nature and meaning of moral language. 

It may be easier to visualize the distinction between the two main branches of ethical theory if we use a flow chart. In normative ethics, we are examining the various theories that are designed to define what the good is and articulate how best to achieve it. This is the action-guiding part of ethics. It's why we call it normative, and it deals with the various moral theories which are all designed to guide our behavior in the world. 

Teleological theories like egoism or utilitarianism claim that the good is best understood in terms of the outcomes of our actions (in other words, the ends are what justify the means; it's the end of our action that is morally important, not how we achieve that end; it's the consequences in other words that determine if we've acted well). 

Now the deontological theories like Kantianism or existentialism, on the other hand, reject consequences as determinative of the good. This group of moral theories claims that the consequences of our actions don't determine goodness (in other words, the ends never justify the means). Rather, there is some intrinsic feature in an action, or perhaps the will of a moral agent, that determines if an action is good or bad, right or wrong. 

Virtue theories like Aristotle's eudaimonism reject both the teleological and deontological approaches to moral obligation, opting instead for an analysis of moral character as the heart of our quest to understand how to be moral. 

So to reiterate: normative ethics is the part of ethical theory that tells us what to do. Metaethics on the other hand is a meta-analysis of the various normative theories. It divides into an investigation into the nature and meaning of moral language, and an analysis of the objectivity or relativity of moral theories. What's important to keep in mind is that metaethics does not give us moral guidance (it is not normative); metaethics tells us *about* normative ethics. 

## Defining Ethics and Morality

Now with this basic understanding of the two different parts of ethical theory in place, we can proceed to explore some more of the basic vocabulary that will be essential to our philosophical investigation of morality. 

Let's begin with the definition of two terms that are often conflated but which actually have distinct meanings in academic philosophy. **Ethics** is the label for the entire branch of axiology that is committed to the investigation of goodness. In particular, ethics investigates the many different and competing moral theories or codes as well as the meta-theories about those codes. Hence we can think of ethics as the science of morality. 

But if ethics is the investigation of morality, then obviously the words ethics and morality are not synonymous, even though people often speak as if they were. So giving an exact definition of **morality** is going to be important so that we understand what exactly it is. But that turns out to be a bit tricky. 

Let's start with this as a preliminary or working definition: *Morality is a code of normative behavior within a group*. If this is accurate, a moral theory (remember it turns out that there's going to be many different moral theories which we'll talk about later on), but a moral theory must define goodness and tell us how to be good. A moral theory is giving us both the "what" and the "how": what we're trying to achieve and how we go about achieving it. 

But this is where the tricky part comes in. It turns out that there are many different types of normative or action-guiding systems in human societies. Let's take a quick look at four of the most common types of normative social systems: 
- A system of **etiquette** lays out the rules of social politeness. 
- **Religion** gives us rules of piety, that is, the appropriate way of interacting with divine beings. 
- **Law** is the normative social system that lays out the rules of criminality (those rules of behavior which if broken tend to carry the heaviest sanctions). 
- So what are the rules of **morality**? We can't say "social interaction" because that would be true of all four systems. We can't say "personal behavior" because all four require individual agents to adhere to the rules of the system. 

Etiquette, religion, law, and morality are all social codes that tell us what to do; they're all normative. So what is the proper domain of morality itself? If we were to say "well, it's the domain of the moral rules", then we've given a circular definition by using the term to be defined within the definition itself. This is what makes it tricky: identifying exactly what the specific domain of morality is as distinct from the other types of social systems. 

Now perhaps if we were to visualize each domain in a Venn diagram, we can achieve some clarity. Note that in our Venn diagram there are both overlapping areas from each domain, but also areas that are unique to each domain. 

As an example, we might say that it's impolite to talk with a mouthful of food. It isn't just that it's impractical to attempt communication while your mouth is otherwise occupied, it's rude. It's a violation of etiquette. But we certainly wouldn't prosecute such boorish behavior in a court of law. Nor would we think it an affront to the gods. Nor would we say it's immoral. It's just rude. 

Next consider the legal requirement to pay your taxes. Failing to do so would be a violation of the criminal code, but it's not rude, it's not impious, nor is it immoral. Some things are clearly just a violation of the criminal law. 

Next consider the obligation to worship. Now in a contemporary civilization, we consider this to be a religious obligation, but not one that should entail criminal sanctions if you fail to comply. Nor would we say that it's rude not to attend religious services, nor would it be immoral. 

What kind of action is it that would fall *only* in the realm of morality and not religion, law, or etiquette? Perhaps something like lying for no reason. Of course outside of perjury or swearing a false oath to the gods, lying doesn't seem to have legal or religious implications. Then again we might consider it a violation of etiquette to lie for no reason. 

There are some actions of course that would be a violation of all four codes. Murder for example is illegal, immoral, impious, and rude. 

But all of this brings us no closer to fully understanding what morality itself is as distinct from the other main normative social systems. So how *should* we define morality? Perhaps it's now more clear why I inserted the adjective "morally" into our preliminary definition, even though this seems to give us a circular definition. Defining morality is tricky, and it's still a matter of debate among ethicists. But perhaps we could say that morality is a normative code of behavior which is distinct in some way from etiquette, religion, and law. But of course we would then want to know, well what is that way in which it's distinct? Perhaps we might find a way forward by thinking about the different forms of sanction that we use in each domain, but what's clear for now is that this is a difficult question. 

It's also clear that ethics is the *study* of morality, whatever it turns out to be. So the term ethics should not be used synonymously with the term morality, at least in the context of academic philosophy. We'll have to leave the discussion here for the moment and move on to look at some other basic terms. Hopefully as we continue to explore and think more carefully about the various competing moral theories, we may come to a better understanding, a clearer understanding, of precisely what morality is. 

## Moral Designators

Now we've already noted that moral systems are axiological, that is they are making value judgments. In each sub-branch of axiology we find unique vocabulary. So associated with the different kinds of judgments being made in ethics, we have a set of terms that we call moral designators. These label the words that indicate that our judgment falls in the domain of morality and not say, aesthetics or political theory. You're of course already familiar with some of these words, but let's get them on the table and see if we can do a better job in defining these. 

We use the word **right** to modify any action which is justified by or is consistent with a particular moral theory. Again keep in mind there's going to be lots of different moral theories and we'll have to come back to them later. So if an action (let's say telling the truth) is obliged in some particular moral theory, then we can say that telling the truth is right. The important thing to remember here is that the word right should only be associated with actions that carry an obligation as specified in the particular moral theory. More simply, right actions are obligatory actions, not mere suggestions. 

The same holds true for the word **wrong**. It's used to identify actions that are contrary to a particular moral framework, and such actions are always forbidden. 

But it's not the case that every action is either going to be right or wrong (obliged or forbidden). There are some actions that are **permissible** within a moral theory. These are actions that are neither commanded nor denied. These are actions that are not inconsistent with a normative code spelled out by a specific theory of the good. Now when it comes to permissible actions, some are going to turn out to be completely neutral in regard to moral theory. If I'm trying to decide which brand of frozen pizza to buy, all things being equal, there are no moral implications to the choice that I make. My choice can not be good nor bad. There may certainly be aesthetic consequences to the choice that I make because one pizza may be better aesthetically than another, but we would never say that my choice has moral consideration. It's a completely neutral action. 

But there are some actions that might be considered morally praiseworthy if I were to perform them, but which I could not be blamed for if I did not perform them. These are called **supererogatory** actions. These are actions that go beyond what would be expected of a moral agent. They're good, but they're not right. Sometimes these are also referred to as altruistic acts. Keep in mind: if an action is right, I'm obliged to do it. But I am under no obligation to perform a supererogatory act, even though it would be proper to give me praise morally if I did perform the action. So good and right don't mean the same thing: every right action will be good, but not every good action will be right. 

## The Core Moral Principle

Now with these terms under our belt, we can turn to the last basic term that I wish to introduce you to in this video. At the heart of every moral theory, there lies a rule or small set of rules that articulate the obligations that arise from that particular moral theory. It is the **moral principle** that reveals how the good is defined in that particular moral framework. Each moral theory will have its own core moral principle. 

For example, we generally recognize three main competing teleological moral theories: hedonism, egoism, and utilitarianism. They all agree that the good is properly defined as a good consequence or a good outcome of a particular action. But they fundamentally differ on what the correct outcome is. 
- Central to hedonism is the principle that everyone should always seek pleasure for themselves. This is what we call the hedonistic principle, and it defines the good as "pleasure for me". 
- Egoism on the other hand defines the good as "happiness for me". So the egoistic principle would be that each person ought to act in a manner that will secure the greatest amount of happiness for themselves. 
- Utilitarians agree with egoists that the good should be defined in terms of happiness, but they disagree about the scope of that good; they universalize the obligation to achieve happiness. The utilitarian principle tells us that everyone ought to seek the greatest amount of happiness for the greatest number of people. 

Each theory holds that the good is the end of the action that is performed, but they differ on what the consequence of our action should be. The moral principle lying at the heart of each theory, whether it's teleological or deontological, will be the embodiment of the definition of the good from that theory's point of view. 

### Five Criteria of an Adequate Moral Principle

But not just any principle of action will be acceptable as a moral principle. After hundreds, indeed thousands of years, ethicists have generally agreed that there are more or less five necessary features an acceptable moral principle ought to have. 

1. **Prescriptivity**: First, since moral actions are obligations (not merely suggestions for action), an adequate moral principle should be stated in the form of an imperative. Moral principles must be prescriptive; they must tell us what we should do, what we ought to do, what we are expected to do. A moral principle is a command, whether affirmative or negative. 
2. **Universality**: Second, moral principles should be universal in scope. They must tell us how *each* moral agent is supposed to act. A moral principle that only applied to some moral agents and not to others would fail to be universalizable and therefore would be inadequate as a moral law. 
3. **Overridingness**: Third, a moral principle is expected to be the primary consideration for action. Of course we have many competing motives when we consider an action; morality should take precedence over the other considerations if there are indeed competing motives. A moral principle therefore should override other considerations, whether they are personal preferences, practical, or legal interests. 
4. **Publicity**: Fourthly, the publicity criterion (which has two dimensions to it) is important for determining whether a proposed principle is going to be an adequate principle in morality. Since a moral code is a social code, it presupposes the interaction of at least two moral agents (or another way of saying it is that morality is public and not private). This reveals the second aspect of the publicity criterion: transparency. Moral codes, as articulated in their core moral principle, should be known to those who are interacting with each other. If your moral code is operating as the overriding motive guiding all of your behavior, others need to know what that guiding principle is. One's guiding principle should never be a secret. Rather, it should be openly displayed so that others can hold you to the obligations that that moral theory requires. If you don't know what moral principle you're operating from, how can you judge or sanction behavior as being consistent with or inconsistent with the principle that you hold? 
5. **Practicability**: Finally, a moral principle needs to meet the standard of practicability. This simply means that a moral principle should never set the requirements for action above what can reasonably be expected of an ordinary moral agent. If we were to propose a moral principle that requires moral perfection, then no one would ever be able to achieve such a standard. So a moral principle needs to be practicable. 

So when we're considering a moral principle in a particular moral theory, we can evaluate it using these five criteria: prescriptivity, universality, overridingness, publicity, and practicability. As we go forward and examine some particular examples of moral theories, we can use these five criteria to help us to decide whether the principle being proposed is a good moral principle. 

## Summary

Let's quickly review some of the main points that we've covered in this short introduction to ethics. 

We've learned that ethics is a sub-branch of axiology and its primary focus is on the concept of goodness. As an academic discipline, ethical theory is an investigation of the various moral codes that have been proposed by different philosophers over the course of intellectual history. We've also discovered that there are two main branches in ethical theory: the normative branch (which focuses on the moral theories themselves), and metaethics (which provides the meta-analysis of the various types of moral theories proposed under normative ethics). 

In trying to define morality, we discovered that it's a social normative system that is distinct in some important way from religion, law, and etiquette, even if it is difficult to pin down exactly what that distinction is. 

And finally, we've discovered that at the core of every moral theory there lies a principle (or perhaps a small set of principles) that articulate the conception of the good which makes that theory distinctive. These principles can be evaluated using the five criteria that help us to determine if a proposed moral principle would be a legitimate candidate moral theory.
