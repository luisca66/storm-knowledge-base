# A Very Brief Introduction to Epistemology (Part 1)

Welcome back to A Little Bit of Philosophy. This is Philosophy 101, Unit 2, Lecture 1, Part 1: A Very Brief Introduction to Epistemology.

We've already learned that academic philosophy is composed of five main subdivisions: metaphysics, axiology, logic, epistemology, and the history of philosophy. In this short video, we'll be focusing on epistemology: the philosophical investigation of knowledge.

## What is Epistemology?

Now, the term epistemology is composed of two ancient Greek words we've already introduced: episteme and logos. You'll recall from our investigation of Plato's Apology of Socrates that episteme is the term that Socrates uses synonymously with sophia to refer to a kind of knowledge that would allow us to live as excellent human beings: the knowledge of virtue. So we can think of episteme as knowledge writ large: broad knowledge, theoretical types of knowledge.

Now, we've also learned that the term logos is the ancient Greek term for argument, or giving a rational account of something, giving an explanation of something, providing a theoretical framework for understanding something. So, we can literally think of epistemology as the branch of philosophy that attempts to provide a rational account of knowledge. 

In this subdivision of academic philosophy, there are three preliminary questions that we are going to attempt to answer: 
1. What is knowledge? 
2. Can we have knowledge? 
3. And if we can, how do we get knowledge?

The first question is essential to address first because if we don't know what we're trying to analyze, how will we know if we're making any progress? Here we're really trying to clearly distinguish between what it means to have knowledge versus what it means to have an opinion. After all, everybody has opinions, but clearly, not everybody has knowledge. 

Now, once we know what knowledge is, or at least get a working definition of knowledge, we can proceed to the second question, which investigates the obstacles that may lie in our way. Given the fact that human beings are limited in their lifespan, sensory capacity, memory, and cognitive ability, is it even possible for us to achieve a state of knowledge? 

Now, if we determine that knowledge is indeed possible for finite creatures like us, we can then go on to address the third and final question: what is the process that will lead us to knowledge? Now, although these questions may seem rather simple, it turns out that the more we struggle with them, the more complex the answers will become.

## What is Knowledge?

Let's begin our analysis by offering some preliminary answers to these three core questions, starting with the question: what is knowledge? 

Remember, we're trying to distinguish between knowing something and having an opinion. Suppose you have a friend who says, "I know that two plus two equals four, but I don't believe it." As you think about their statement, you would be profoundly puzzled. How can they know something and yet fail to believe it? This realization tells us something essential about what knowledge is. Belief is an essential component of knowledge; we can't know something without also believing it. However, simply believing something doesn't mean that you know it. After all, people believe lots of things that they don't actually know. 

To clarify this further, let's take the case of "stick figure person." Let's suppose that stick figure person has a belief in their mind: they believe that there is a bee. The belief that there is a bee is a mental event, it's a thing inside their mind, but this is just a belief. It can't be knowledge unless there actually is a bee. In other words, the belief must be true. So beyond holding a belief, that belief must also be true if we're going to have knowledge. 

But when we think a little bit more carefully about this, we can see that simply holding a belief that's true doesn't quite get us up to knowledge either. I might, for example, believe that it's raining in Dublin right now, and if it is indeed raining in Dublin right now, my belief would be true. But we wouldn't say that I know it's raining in Dublin. For that, we need something else. For that, we need something extra: we need to add evidence to support the belief. What I would need is a logos, a reason, a justification. 

Justification is what ties the beliefs in our mind to the world. Justification is the argument or support or warrant that we give to our belief that demonstrates, that shows, that it's true. Now, if I can provide evidence for my belief that's adequate, then I would have true justified belief. Then, and only then, it seems, can I say that I have knowledge. 

Now, even that formula, true justified belief, may have some weaknesses, but for our present purposes, it will do. And also, as a historical note, it was Plato who first put this formula of knowledge as true justified belief forward as at least a working definition of knowledge, although even Plato too had some concerns about whether it completely captured what was necessary in order to have knowledge.

Now with a working definition of knowledge as true justified belief, we can proceed to our second question in epistemology: can we have knowledge? Can we possess knowledge? Can we achieve true justified belief? 

As you might expect, there's obviously going to be at least two possible answers at this point: yes, and no. It will turn out to be a little bit more complicated than that, but for right now, let's start off with these first two possibilities. Dogmatism is the position in epistemology that answers our question affirmatively. Dogmatists are philosophers who believe that it is possible to provide adequate justification to move a belief from the realm of opinion into the realm of knowledge. 

On the other hand, there are philosophers who disagree. There are some philosophers who are going to claim that our cognitive or sensory limitations make adequate justification impossible. This position is generally known as skepticism. 

Now, on closer analysis, it's going to turn out that these positions are not binary, but for now, we can think of those who are more optimistic about our chances of achieving knowledge as being on the dogmatic side, and those who are more pessimistic on the skeptical side. And if one is skeptical about knowledge, then there's really no more work to be done. We can't have knowledge, so why worry about it? But for those on the dogmatic side, for those who think it may be possible to achieve knowledge, our third question becomes critically important.

## How Do We Get Knowledge?

If knowledge is possible, then how do we get it? Here again, we'll find that philosophers disagree on the correct answer. 

One position amongst dogmatists is that knowledge can only be achieved by applying reason to the ideas in our mind. That is, rationally ordering those ideas. We can call this rational reflection on our ideas. But given that the skeptics have raised doubts about the reliability of the ideas we get from our senses, how can such dubious ideas ever rise to the level of complete justification? 

The answer from the rationalists, the first approach we'll examine, is that we can't. There must be some ideas which are beyond the doubt that can be raised concerning our acquired beliefs. There must be some ideas that are not acquired. These are called innate ideas: ideas that we're born with, ideas that are a necessary part of the mind itself. Rationalists believe that when we're born, we already have a basic set of ideas in our mind, like a pre-formatted hard drive. Now, the origin of these innate ideas is itself a matter of debate amongst the rationalists, but wherever they come from, they're considered to be free from the need of ordinary justification, and that's the critical part. On the other hand, the beliefs we acquire through experience, through our senses for example, will need to be justified in relation to those innate ideas in order to reach a level of knowledge. 

Now, there's another group of dogmatists who completely reject the existence of innate ideas. Empiricism is the dogmatic view that we're born as a blank slate, and all of the ideas that we have in our mind are acquired through some form of experience, or abstraction from our experience. After we begin having experiences with the world, our minds are more like an unformatted hard drive when we're born, according to the empiricists. 

Now, if this sounds to you like the nature-nurture debate in psychology, you'd be right. That debate comes from this dispute in epistemology. It's important to note before moving on, that when we're talking about ideas or beliefs, that is, fully formed cognitions in our minds, we're not talking about instincts. Empiricists would not deny that we may be born with instincts, and when rationalists talk about innate ideas, they're certainly not referring to instincts. And whatever those instinct things are, they're certainly not ideas or beliefs. You breathe and digest and regulate your blood pressure without thinking about it. Epistemology is interested in the "thinky things", not the non-"thinky things" or processes that may be happening in the brain. We'll come back to this notion between the different kinds of mental events a little bit later.

Now, given that there's a debate between empiricists and rationalists, and that debate turns on the difference between acquired and innate ideas, a little bit more vocabulary will be helpful in distinguishing between different kinds of ideas and the knowledge that can be derived from them. 

Philosophers have adopted the Latin phrase *a priori* to refer to any idea that is not dependent on sensory experience for its cognizability. The phrase literally means "from before", and refers to any belief or piece of knowledge that can be acquired in the absence of immediate sensory experience. 

Perhaps one of the simplest examples of this would be from geometry. Take for example a point: a singular, infinitely small region in space. Now take a second point, an infinitely small distinct region in space. Between point A and point B, we can have a line, and if we add a third point that is not on that line, we get a triangle. So what does a triangle smell like? How does a triangle taste? What does a triangle sound like? Of course, the idea "triangle" has no sensory qualities at all, it's just an idea. And this is the kind of thing philosophers are talking about when they use the phrase *a priori* to modify an idea or knowledge. It's an idea, or it is knowledge that can be grasped without direct sensory experience, without sensory data content.

Now, most of the ideas that we discover in our minds are not *a priori* in nature. Most are *a posteriori*. I want you to think about an apple. The idea certainly contains a shape, like "triangle" which is only shape, but there's much more data in your idea of an apple. There's the redness of the skin, the green of the leaf, the brown of the stem. And all of that is merely visual data. You can also imagine the fragrance as you bring the apple close to your mouth, the smoothness of the texture of the skin, the sound - the crunch as you bite into it - as well as the sweet but slightly tart taste in your mouth. Your idea of an apple contains all of this information, all of which is dependent on your five senses. This is an *a posteriori* idea, an idea that can only be cognized after the sensory experience you've had with an apple. 

That's the difference between *a priori* and *a posteriori* ideas. The latter are dependent on sense data, the former are not. Now, when a rationalist claims that we have some innate ideas in our mind, they're not talking about *a posteriori* ideas, they're talking about *a priori* ideas. However, it's really important to keep in mind that rationalists don't need *all* *a priori* ideas to be innate in order to acquire a knowledge, just *some* of them. So while innate ideas will always be *a priori* in nature, it is not the case that every *a priori* idea will be innate. In other words, the words innate and *a priori* are not synonymous terms.

## Summary

Okay, let's review what we've covered so far. We've discovered that there are three basic questions in epistemology, which is the philosophical investigation of knowledge. 

The first question attempts to elicit a clear demarcation between knowledge and opinion, and we discovered that a good working definition is "true justified belief." Now, once we have this working definition in place, we can proceed to the second question. Here we discovered two possible positions: dogmatism and skepticism. The dogmatist believes that knowledge is possible, while the skeptic is less optimistic. 

The third basic question of epistemology applies to those who think that knowledge is possible, the dogmatists in other words. Here we noted two distinct approaches to getting knowledge, based on how we acquire the ideas that we are to turn into knowledge. The rationalists believe beyond the ideas we acquire through our senses, that we also have a set of innate ideas which will be *a priori* in nature, that will provide a framework for addressing the skeptic's concern about our limited sensory abilities. On the other hand, there are the empiricists, who don't believe in the existence of innate ideas at all. So if we're going to have knowledge, according to the empiricists, we'll just have to make do with the ideas we acquire through experience.
