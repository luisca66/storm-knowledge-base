# PHI 101 U01 L05 The Historical Background to Sokrates: The Rise of Athens (Part 2)

Welcome back to *The Historical Background to Socrates: The Rise and Fall of the Athenian Empire*.

This short two-part video series is designed to provide you with the general historical and cultural overview of 5th-century Athens to help you read and understand Plato's *Apology* more effectively.

In this video, we'll focus on imperial Athens under Pericles, the rise of sophism, the Peloponnesian War, and the events immediately preceding the trial of Socrates in 399 BCE.

In our last video, we noted that the people we think of as the ancient Greeks were actually a new culture that arose in the aftermath of the Bronze Age collapse in a period we called the Dark Age. It's called dark because for nearly 400 years after the Bronze Age ended there are few, if any, written records, and the massive population displacement, and indeed population decrease, across the Near East caused a disruption of the material record that would usually, along with the historical records, tell us about the people who lived at that particular time, what they were doing, what their political interests were, what their alliances were, wars, famines, and so on.

It's almost as if someone suddenly turned off the lights in a large room. We quickly lose sight of where things are, and that's exactly what a Dark Age is like. Things suddenly go dark, the material record and the historical record come to an abrupt end.

But as with all Dark Ages, this one would also come to an end, and it's followed by what we call the Archaic, literally the old age or period. This is the period when the Greeks became literate, when their population rose to a level that required new and innovative social organizations like the *polis*, when they began trading with non-Greek speaking peoples, and when they were forced to deal with the rising problem of overpopulation.

The newly formed Greek city-states began sending out groups of colonists, which soon expanded the Greek world, or Greater Greece, to such an extent that Plato would much later describe the Greeks as a bunch of frogs sitting around the edge of the pond, that pond of course being the Mediterranean. And it was the expansion of Greater Greece that would, at the end of the Archaic period, bring the Greeks into economic and military conflict with the dominant empire of the day, Persia.

The Persian Wars were so important in the history of the Greeks that they mark the end of the Archaic period and the beginning of what we call the Classical Age, which is the apex of Greek civilization.

This is the period of Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle, Euripides, Aeschylus, Sappho, and Sophocles, Herodotus and Thucydides, Phidias and Praxiteles. This brief 150-year period encompassed most of what we today think of as ancient Greece, and brief as it was, the effects of this period still reverberate throughout the world today.

And at the center of this cultural flowering of the Classical period was the city-state of Athens.

Now our last video ended with the close of the Archaic period as the Greeks struggled to maintain their independence from the expanding influence of the Persian hegemony that dominated the Near East. We saw how the Athenians stood nearly alone against the might of the Persians on the field of Marathon and were victorious despite the odds. We saw in the Second Persian War how the Greeks came together to fend off a full-scale invasion of Europe from Xerxes, and how the intrigues of Themistocles forced a confrontation between the Greek and Persian navies, changing the course of the war. We also noted how those very intrigues may have led to his fall from grace, as he was ostracized from the city after the war, essentially ending his political influence in the city of Athens.

We pick up the story with the formation of the Delian and Peloponnesian Leagues, the two military alliances the Greeks formed after the defeat of Persia to defend against a possible third imperial invasion.

There had of course always been various military alliances between the Greek *poleis*, but they were never permanent, nor were they nearly as global in their reach. The Persian Wars had changed the way the Greeks looked at the world, both reinforcing a cultural self-perception that to be Greek was to be special, and also the belief that anybody that wasn't Greek, and that essentially meant anybody that didn't speak the Greek language, is a barbarian, a non-Greek.

And nowhere was this new perspective, this new distinction between what it meant to be Greek and what it meant to be non-Greek, more in evidence than in the city of Athens.

## Periclean Athens

Classical Athens can't be separated from the leadership of Pericles, perhaps the greatest statesman in all of Athenian history.

As a member of the wealthy and powerful Alcmaeonid family, one of the chief noble families of Athens, known as the *eupatridae*, literally those with good fathers, we might call them nobles or aristocrats, Pericles seems to have entered public life in his early to mid-thirties and quickly established himself as a leading orator and politician.

Unlike many others of his class, Pericles seemed to understand the power of the lower classes and worked to establish himself as a representative of their interests. First, leading the ostracism of Cimon, who had led the aristocratic faction that had ostracized Themistocles immediately after the Second Persian War. Pericles also led an effort to ensure that poor citizens could attend the theater for free. He lowered the property requirements for holding high public office, and also, and perhaps most importantly, instituted payment for those who served on juries.

Jury duty was randomly selected, just like it is in our society today, but in Athens you had to serve jury duty for an entire year while the courts were in session. You were required to go and hear cases. So obviously, if you're not being paid for that duty, you can't go to work at the same time. So what tended to happen is only those who were independently wealthy could actually take the time to do their civic duty by serving on a jury.

By providing payment for members of the jury, Pericles was opening the door to those citizens who otherwise would have been excluded from this form of public service. Even though they were obligated to do it, they couldn't go and do it because they had to go to work.

All of these actions were designed to kick open the doors to participation in the Athenian democracy by the lower classes. Even though Athens was nominally a democracy, it was a de facto oligarchy, controlled by the wealthy *eupatrid* families.

Like Themistocles, Pericles understood that the power of Athens lay with its navy, and the navy was dependent on those lower classes whose economic interests didn't lie with traditional sources of wealth like land ownership. You see, it was from the poor classes that the sailors for the Athenian fleet were recruited. So being on the side of the democratic party, opposing the *eupatrids*, the aristocrats, was the key to his political success.

And by 461 BCE he would become the dominant statesman of the city and would hold that influence until his death 32 years later.

It was under his leadership that Athens would become a true democracy, or what today political theorists call a direct democracy. It is a city ruled by the people, not just the wealthy and powerful citizens of the *polis* representing everyone else.

But Pericles was not only responsible for fostering the first true democracy in world history, he also oversaw the rebuilding of the city itself. You'll recall that Xerxes had burned the city to the ground during the Second Persian War, including the temples on the sacred Acropolis. It was under Pericles that the Acropolis would be rebuilt, leaving an architectural legacy that still astonishes those who visit today.

But the infrastructure project that Pericles led was incredibly expensive, and although Athens was a wealthy city, it was going to need an influx of capital to realize Pericles's grandiose dream of the ideal Greek *polis*.

Fortunately, the Delian League would help make that dream a reality.

## The Delian League and Athenian Empire

Now the Delian League was composed of the city-states around the Aegean that were traditional naval states, led by Athens and the territory of Attica on the Greek mainland. Most of the member states were among the colonies of Ionia liberated by the Greek victory in the Second Persian War.

The league's treasury was kept on the island of Delos in the southern Aegean. Delos was a sacred island to the Greeks, and the temple of Apollo on the small island provided a sanctuary where cities could hold assets without fear of theft. No one would steal from a temple.

But what would prevent non-Greeks, be they Persians or pirates, from looting the sacred territory? This was actually part of Pericles's argument to the allies when he lobbied them to move the treasury from Delos to Athens.

Once the Athenians had control of the treasury, it wasn't hard to engage in some creative accounting, redirecting some of the funds in the Delian League treasury that were supposed to be going to build ships and train sailors to the Athenian reconstruction fund.

Pericles also convinced the allies to allow Athens to control the actual production of the ships instead of each city building their own and then contributing them to the fleet. If league members would merely pay into the treasury and leave the manufacturing and training to the Athenians, who had the largest infrastructure for such an undertaking, this agreement lifted a burden from the member states, but it also gave Athens the upper hand over their allies, who were now dependent upon the Athenians for providing them with defense.

It wouldn't be too long until the Athenians began imposing a kind of tribute on their allies for managing all the logistics of the league's treasury and ship production.

In essence, Athens was beginning to see itself as the first of the states in the league, and the other members not so much as allies but as subordinates. And as you can imagine, this did not go over well with the other members of the league.

Not only was Pericles responsible for establishing Athenian hegemony over the allies of the Delian League, rebuilding the infrastructure of the city, and reforming the political practice of the city to make it a true democracy, he also needed to quickly raise the educational level of the citizens of Athens.

There was nothing like public education as we would understand that today. So how do you take a whole class of people who had no tradition of political participation and give them the tools that they need, especially the skill of public speaking, to effectively participate in what is now a fully democratic system of government?

The answer was to invite the best and brightest teachers from around Greater Greece to come to Athens to teach the people how to be good citizens.

## The Sophists

Those teachers were known as *sophistai*, or wise men. These freelance professors would, for a fee, teach the young men of the city everything they needed to know about how to be influential in Athenian politics, or so they claimed.

As we'll see more clearly in Plato's *Apology of Socrates*, it's not entirely clear that those wise men were really wise at all. In fact, one of the main themes of Plato's work is to clearly delineate between those who are thought to be wise, whether by themselves or others, and those like Socrates who seek to be wise while also being fully aware that they haven't yet arrived.

But whatever the case may be, Pericles's invitation caused a flood of teachers to enter the city, where they would set up shop in the *agora* to demonstrate their oratory skills in order to drum up business.

There would be many of these sophists, or sages, that would come to the city of Athens in the 5th century, and none more famous than Protagoras of Abdera. While their individual specializations may have varied, they all claimed that they could teach virtue, or human excellence, that morality was relative, that the traditional stories of the gods, while instructive, shouldn't be taken too literally, and that success in public life was dependent upon one's ability to convince their fellow citizens through the art of debate.

And in a city like Athens, it was this last point that was of critical concern. To learn the art of rhetoric was the key to political power.

The sophists soon gathered followings among the young and aspiring men of Athens, and they would teach a whole generation of politicians how to be excellent public speakers, even if they didn't become experts in the subjects upon which they spoke.

## The Peloponnesian War

This brings us back to the Athenian grip on the Delian League. Athens was becoming a true naval empire, riding on the support of their allies, or subjects depending upon your point of view.

You see, the new and improved democracy of Athens didn't really extend to the other members of the Delian League. In fact, cities that complained about Athens's imperial behavior toward their allies found themselves forced to accept the will of Athens or face their wrath.

And when the Athenians began meddling in the political affairs of cities in the west of Greece, cities that were traditionally part of the Peloponnesian League, it began to look as if Athens intended to extend its dominance over all of Greece.

The Athenians were beginning to behave like the Persians, and even think like them too. After all, hadn't their victory at Marathon demonstrated that the Athenians were favored by the gods? Wasn't it Themistocles's strategy to trick both the Persians and the Greeks into the battle of Salamis that ended up saving Greece from the Persians? Wasn't their prosperity, their wealth in the post-war period, evidence that the Athenian way of life was superior to other Greek cities? Surely these successes made it manifest that Athens was destined to be an empire.

Why shouldn't they export their form of government to other places by supporting democratic coups in traditional oligarchic cities? After all, who was going to stop them?

It was precisely this attitude that led the Athenians to believe that their wealth and power made it right for them to do as they pleased. And it was that attitude that would finally lead Sparta and their allies in the Peloponnesian League to declare war on Athens to curb their expansion.

The Peloponnesian War, or wars, as there are several distinct phases of military activity, was the great civil war of the Greeks, and it would mark the beginning of the end of the Classical Age. The conflict would last nearly 30 years, and few cities would escape the devastation that would come in its wake.

Now at the outset of the war, it was clear that Athens had the upper hand, both in territory, wealth, and material resources, over Sparta and her allies. On paper, the Athenians were a sure bet to win. But wars aren't fought on paper, and fortune doesn't always favor the rich.

Now the story of the war is absolutely fascinating and complicated, and we don't really have time to go into it here. To be sure, the Athenians had the upper hand through much of the conflict. Indeed, the Spartans even begged the Athenians to accept their surrender at one point. But the new class of politicians educated under the sophists, so good at giving public speeches and demagoguery, would convince the Assembly that Athens deserved nothing less than absolute victory. The Spartans should not be given favorable terms, since they started the war in the first place.

And early in the conflict, Pericles, the unparalleled statesman and strategist, had died of an illness that swept through the city. So the leadership of the democracy had forever passed to that new generation of politicians.

It was their uncompromising attitude and belief in their own wisdom that would ultimately lead to the city's defeat in 404 BCE.

Stripped of their fleet, shorn of their defenses, the city of Athens was humiliated before their former allies and their enemies to boot.

To add insult to injury, the democracy was overthrown in a coup by a group of Athenian aristocrats, the children and grandchildren of the nobles who had been humiliated during the Periclean reforms that allowed ordinary people access to the levers of power.

What the city needed, they believed, was a return to the oligarchic leadership of the good old days. After all, it was the democracy that had destroyed the city.

But once in power, the Thirty Tyrants, as they came to be known, immediately set about enriching themselves and their friends and exacting revenge on anyone they believed had ever harmed them or their families. They were so ruthless and transparent in their corruption that after only a year the whole city rose up against them and overthrew the oligarchy of the Thirty and reinstated the democracy.

Now, to prevent a cycle of vendettas from running wild in the city, for by this time almost everyone had a grudge against everybody else, the Assembly passed a general amnesty forbidding anyone from being prosecuted for crimes of treason for what they might have done during the rule of the Thirty Tyrants or indeed through the Peloponnesian War.

## Socrates in the Aftermath

And this brings us directly to the case of Socrates. If Plato's account of the trial of Socrates holds any historical veracity, it is clear that the Athenians thought of him as a sophist whom they've now come to blame for their loss of empire and their public humiliation.

But unlike the sophists, Socrates is an Athenian citizen. He has no place else to go. The sophists had all left town when the war ended and when the city was plunged into political chaos. But he was left behind, and some of his fellow citizens saw him as an existential threat to the fragile democracy newly re-established.

But the amnesty made it impossible for them to charge him with treason. They'll just have to find some other way of getting rid of this pest.

As you'll see when you read the *Apology*, Socrates mounts a Herculean defense, but the cards were all stacked against him and his conviction was all but inevitable.

As we conclude this video, we must note two characters who more than any other probably led to Socrates's inevitable doom.

Critias was Plato's step-uncle, and probably how the younger man Plato came to be introduced to Socrates in the latter years of the Peloponnesian War. But in his youth Critias, an Athenian aristocrat, was a follower of Socrates, like many others who found the antics of the professed lover of wisdom appealing to their youthful iconoclasm. But when he grew up, he would become a leading politician during the war, and in the post-war period he would find himself leading the Thirty Tyrants in their reign of terror.

He, of course, was killed during the democratic rebellion against the oligarchy and could not be held accountable for his actions. But the Athenians couldn't help but wonder how a fine aristocratic youth like Critias had turned into such a villain. He must have fallen in with bad company. Who led him astray? And who did he hang out with when he was young and impressionable? Why, it's that crusty old Socrates. He must be to blame for Critias's corruption.

Perhaps the most infamous of Socrates's followers was another young aristocrat, Alcibiades. He too was a follower of Socrates in his youth, and he too would betray his city as an adult. Alcibiades was a general, perhaps one of the best Athens ever produced, but he would betray the city to Sparta during the ill-fated Sicilian expedition, which marked the climax of the Peloponnesian War in 415 BCE.

As you read the *Apology of Socrates* by Plato, you must keep in mind the rich and tragic history of Athens during Socrates's life. They no sooner became an empire than they were defeated by the very people that they sought to dominate. They went from being the richest, most powerful, and influential of all the Greek city-states down to ignoble defeat.

And when that happens, people naturally look for a reason, a cause, an explanation to help it all make sense. Trapped in the crosshairs of this soul-searching was the enigmatic Socrates, a man who suffered no fools, refused to accept the social conventions of his day, and who was merciless in his cross-examination of those who claimed to know what they were doing, which basically included the entire male population of the city. They all thought they were experts, after all. It's a democracy, and democracy is premised on the idea that the ordinary person must know what is best, not just for themselves, but for the society at large.

So what we see is that Socrates had few friends in the city, and if a scapegoat was needed in order to restore order, he fit the bill.
