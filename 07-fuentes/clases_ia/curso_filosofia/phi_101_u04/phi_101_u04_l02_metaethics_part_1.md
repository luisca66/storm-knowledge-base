# Metaethics (Part 1)

Welcome back to A Little Bit of Philosophy. This is Philosophy 101, Unit 4, Lecture 2: An Overview of Metaethics, Part 1. 

You'll recall from our last video that ethical theory divides into two main branches: normative ethics and metaethics. Normative ethics is the investigation of the various different kinds of specific moral theories that tell us how we should act, how we should live our lives, and all of these various theories can be organized under three headings as teleological theories, deontological theories, or virtue theories. 

Under metaethics, we find two subdivisions: semantics and descriptivism. In this video we'll be focusing on descriptivism. 

## Descriptivism and Moral Objectivity

Descriptivism is the part of metaethics that explores whether or not the good, or morality, is something we discover about the world or something we create in the world. Is morality objective like mathematics, or is it relative like etiquette and law which differs from place to place or time to time? There are of course arguments on both sides and we'll briefly sketch out the various views in what follows. 

But let's start with the idea that morality is objective. If it turns out that there are indeed moral facts about the world, then those facts will either be unconstrained (absolute and universal) or they may be limited or constrained in some way. 

### Moral Absolutism vs. Moral Objectivism

**Moral absolutism** holds that the good and therefore morality exists as an independent and unconstrained fact about the universe and cannot be limited or altered by us. That is, the rules always apply no matter what. 

**Moral objectivism**, on the other hand, is less radical. It holds that while morality is indeed objective, there may be some constraints that apply to moral obligations, like two conflicting moral obligations that might arise from the same moral principle. 

Consider the classic case proposed by the moral absolutist Immanuel Kant. Suppose that a killer comes to your house and demands to know the whereabouts of a person you're hiding inside. Let's also suppose that you hold a moral theory that entails that you should not lie, and you also hold a moral theory that tells you that you ought to protect the innocent. What should you do? Kant would say that you should not lie, because your moral principle cannot be overridden by other facts, even if those facts are part of a competing moral theory. True, you face a moral tragedy, but it's not of your making; you're bound by the moral law no matter what. That's moral absolutism. 

A moral objectivist, on the other hand, would say that one moral obligation might take precedence over another in circumstances like this. Protecting an innocent person might be of greater moral importance than truth-telling in this case. That's the difference between these two views. Absolutism does not allow for overriding circumstances when it comes to your moral obligations; they are absolute. The objectivist, however, does allow for ordering or ranking moral obligations if competing moral obligations were to arise. 

Both views hold that morality is an objective feature of the world and not something we invent for ourselves. The question is whether or not there are ever exceptions. 

### Natural vs. Non-Natural Facts

Now you may be wondering what would make morality objective in the first place. There are two different kinds of facts that could make morality objective: natural or non-natural facts. 

A utilitarian like Jeremy Bentham or John Stuart Mill would point to the **natural fact** that suffering is bad and happiness is good. Therefore, we should always seek to minimize suffering and we should always promote happiness in our actions. Suffering and happiness are examples of natural facts. 

Immanuel Kant, on the other hand, argues that moral obligation is derived from reason, which transcends the world of material facts and biology and would therefore bind any being capable of rational cognition, regardless of their biological composition. Your ability to experience suffering is not necessary in order to recognize moral obligation. The laws and structures of reason, in other words, would be **non-natural facts** about the universe. 

So one branch of descriptivism (objectivism) holds that the good and morality are objective features of the world, and the obligations derived from them will be either absolute or constrained under certain circumstances. 

## Moral Relativism

The alternative to moral objectivism is moral relativism. If the good and morality are not objective facts, if they are not discovered, then they must be created. The question is: created by whom? To whom is morality relative? There are two main possibilities for us to consider. On the one hand, moral obligation might be determined by each individual, or on the other, it might be determined by some particular group. 

### Subjectivism

**Subjectivism** is the radical form of moral relativism which claims that each and every individual moral agent must decide for themselves what they think they ought to do. I decide what's good for me and you decide what's good for you. If we happen to agree on what we ought to do, so much the better. But if we don't, my decision doesn't constrain you, nor does your decision constrain me. On this view morality is strictly left up to the individual. Full stop. 

### Conventionalism

**Conventionalism**, on the other hand, holds that the good and moral obligations are determined by some particular group. For example, morality might be familia-centric, where the norms of moral behavior and obligation are determined by an individual family. More broadly, morality might be determined by one's culture, yielding an ethnocentric moral code. Or we might consider morality to be the product of an historical period, or even the product of the corporate agreement of the human species as a whole. 

So whether morality is an individual choice or some form of social agreement, all forms of moral relativism hold that we create moral obligations, we do not discover them. Morality is relative, not objective. 

Again, it may help to visualize the various positions that fall under the heading of descriptivism. Relativism divides into conventionalism on the one hand and subjectivism on the other, and there are many differing forms of conventionalism, whether it's family-centered, culturally-centered, historically-centered, or whether it's human-centered (species-centered). 

## The Rise of Moral Relativism

Now, most people today—though not necessarily most philosophers—tend to think of morality as being relative, and it's worth spending a few minutes exploring why this is the case, and whether or not it's warranted. The late philosopher Louis Pojman convincingly argued that there are three main reasons relativism has come to dominate the popular view of morality: 
1. Our awareness and rejection of ethnocentrism. 
2. The declining influence of religion in Western culture. 
3. A fallacious juxtaposition of moral absolutism against conventionalism. 

### 1. The Rejection of Ethnocentrism

Let's start with the fact that today we generally reject ethnocentrism, that is, the uncritical belief in the inherent superiority of one's own culture. The period immediately following the Second World War marks the beginning of the post-colonial era, when European nations that had since the late Renaissance expanded their hegemony over much of the world began to lose control of their colonies. The Second World War had devastated the economy and infrastructure of Western Europe, and the new Cold War diverted military and political focus to the perceived threat of international communism, making it practically impossible for Europeans to maintain their colonies in Africa and Asia. 

As more and more colonies achieved independence, social attitudes toward formerly colonized populations began to shift. While colonialism was primarily driven by economic self-interest, it was always bolstered by the ethnocentric belief in the inherent superiority of Western culture. As colonies achieved independence and demonstrated their ability to rule themselves, it forced a recognition and re-evaluation of the very ethnocentricity which had eased the conscience of Europeans who for hundreds of years had systematically stripped their fellow human beings of their cultures, their land, their resources, and in many cases, their very lives. People began to reject the myth of Western superiority, and in turn to embrace the idea of cultural diversity. 

This shift in worldview was bolstered by a rising awareness of cultural diversity caused by the increasing popularity of academic anthropology. As more and more people gained access to higher education in the latter half of the 20th century, they were exposed to the methodology and findings of anthropologists who had long challenged the ethnocentric assumptions of Western culture. The result of these changes was in part a shift toward the idea that perhaps the Western view of morality was only one among many possible moral worldviews, and that emerging global awareness should prevent us from assuming a position of moral superiority over others. 

### 2. The Decline of Religion

The second claim in Pojman's argument is that the rise in moral relativism is tied to the decline of the role of religion in Western society. It's difficult for us today to imagine—that is from our perspective of religious diversity and religious tolerance—that there was once a time when there was only one religious system that dominated all of Western culture. But from the end of the Roman Empire in the late 5th century all the way up to the 16th century, Christianity (Roman Catholicism more precisely) was essentially the only religion in Western Europe. 

And throughout the Middle Ages, the Church was not just a spiritual or religious institution; as the sole surviving civil institution from the Roman Empire, it played a fundamental role in politics, economics, education, and socialization for nearly a thousand years. It also provided a uniform moral worldview, as there were no alternatives to challenge its hegemony. 

But in 1517 Martin Luther successfully challenged the monolithic authority of the Church and started a process of religious fragmentation that extends right down to the present. Of course Luther's intent had only been to reform what he saw as abuses of authority in the Church, but the Protestant Reformation which he unwittingly started would re-introduce religious pluralism in Europe not seen since the 4th century. Even if all the new religious sects that emerged after Luther were primarily versions of Christianity, the fact was there was no longer a single voice of moral authority in Europe. The Catholic Church could no longer claim exclusive authority in matters of morality. 

The rise of the new science only made matters worse. As modern science advanced, there was an increasing need for institutions of learning to discover new things, as well as to pass on what had been discovered. In the medieval world of course, the Church had exclusive control over education in Europe, but no longer. New universities and scientific societies arose in the Protestant parts of Europe, which challenged the parochial curricula of Catholic universities, further diminishing the role of religion in education. 

By the time the 18th century rolled around, the scientific revolution gave birth to the industrial revolution which would further challenge the centrality of religion in Western society. Prior to the industrial revolution most people lived a rural life centered in agriculture, but the automation capable in the new factories meant that people could earn more than ever before if they left the countryside and moved to the growing industrial cities. This challenged the role that religion had traditionally played in socialization. Whether Protestant or Catholic, in the pre-industrial agrarian culture of Europe, churches were a necessary institution for social interaction and news of the world. But as more and more people moved into cities, religion was less and less necessary as an outlet for social interaction. 

What we see beginning with the Protestant Reformation of the 16th century is a steady and sustained decline in the role that religion played in the lives of ordinary people, so that by the time we reach the present, religion is almost exclusively a spiritual institution, and a voluntary one at that. Religion can no longer provide a single authoritative voice in determining morality as it once did. 

### 3. False Disjunction

The final premise in Pojman's argument regards how the debate between moral relativism and moral objectivism is often discussed. He claims that the issue is often framed as a false disjunction strictly between some form of conventionalism on the one hand, and moral absolutism on the other. 

This is a fallacious construction since there are multiple versions of conventionalism, and one needn't be a moral absolutist to believe that morality is objective. But if someone were confronted with a choice between the recognition of moral diversity on the one hand and the claim that there is one and only one true moral system, it seems more rational to choose the former rather than the latter. But again, since this is a fallacious argument, we don't have to take the bait and choose between only two options. 

## Logical Arguments for and against Moral Relativism

So to summarize, moral relativism has become the popular view of morality for at least three reasons: our rejection of ethnocentrism and the desire to be more tolerant of views that differ from our own, the decline of religion in Western culture, and the false disjunction between conventionalism and absolutism. But setting aside these historical and sociological factors, what's the *logical* argument in favor of moral relativism? After all, just because people believe something doesn't make it so. 

In its most general form, moral relativism is the view that there are no objective or universal moral standards. This conclusion is supported by two premises we'll call the diversity and dependency thesis. 

### The Diversity and Dependency Theses

The **diversity thesis** holds that moral codes differ from culture to culture, and indeed from one historical period to another. This thesis is established by the work of cultural anthropologists who have diligently worked to examine the differences between the many different cultures that we encounter around the world. Moral diversity is a fact; there are many different moral codes in the world. 

But by itself that doesn't warrant the conclusion that there is *no* objective moral code. It could very well be that there are many differing moral codes and that one of them turns out to be correct. Or it could be that there are many different moral codes and none of them are correct. In order to conclude moral relativism, we need to add a second premise to the argument: that all morality derives its authority from social acceptance. This is what we call the **dependency thesis**. 

If it is true that a moral code can only be binding if accepted by a particular group, and if it is true that different groups accept different moral standards, then it would follow that morality is not and indeed probably cannot be objective. But is this argument compelling? It could certainly be put into valid deductive form, but would it be sound? 

### The Problems with Subjectivism

Now few if any would argue with the diversity thesis; it seems to be well established by good social science. But what about the dependency thesis? Is it true that the moral authority of a moral system is derived solely from its acceptance by a group? To see why this may not be the case, let's begin by examining the most extreme form of relativism: moral subjectivism. 

Remember that it's the view or the form of relativism that claims that each individual determines for themselves what the moral code should be. If we think carefully about this position, it becomes clear that this will lead to absurd conclusions. 

A moral code must be normative: it must tell me what to do. If I get to decide which code I will accept, the imperative force of the code disappears. The moral code isn't telling me what I should and shouldn't do, *I'm* telling me what I should and shouldn't do. On a subjectivist account of morality, the very idea of morality becomes vacuous. What I ought to do is what I want to do, and I ought to do it because I want to. This of course is nothing more than simply saying "I'm going to do whatever I want." There is nothing normative informing my actions; there is simply my will to do as I please. 

Secondly, on the subjectivist account of morality it would seem to be impossible to engage in moral criticism. Since morality has been reduced to everyone doing whatever they want, how could we claim that anyone is acting immorally? Again, criticism and sanction are essential parts of any normative system including morality. If I can't sanction someone for violation of the normative code, in what sense is it a normative code at all? 

Since it would seem that subjectivism leads to absurd consequences, it should be rejected. 

### The Problems with Conventionalism

But what about the more moderate form of moral relativism: conventionalism? You'll recall that it says that morality is determined by the agreement of some particular group, not the individual moral agent. But just a little reflection shows that conventionalism will fare no better than subjectivism. 

Firstly, if it is the group or society that determines the moral code, we must be able to identify what counts as a sufficient group to be called a society. How many people are necessary in order to form a society? Do we need 100 people? Or 50 people? Or perhaps just two? And if a society could be two people or more that happen to agree, then we're almost right back to subjectivism. Sure we've cut the number of possible moral codes in half, but it still leaves us with an unwieldy number of codes. 

Secondly, just as with subjectivism, how would intercultural moral judgment be possible if one were a conventionalist? If morality is determined by each society (whatever that turns out to be), then it would be impossible for one society to make moral criticisms of another, and surely this would be just as absurd as it was with subjectivism. 

Thirdly, how would moral reform be possible within a conventionalist framework? If morality is determined by the agreement of the group, how would it be possible for the group to change its view over time? Anyone who proposed a moral reform would by definition be articulating an immoral position, since morality is nothing more than what the group believes it to be. If we think about the great moral reformers of the past (people who argued against the accepted moral standards of their community), a conventionalist would have to say that their position, since it differs from the accepted standard of the group, was immoral. And yet we have to recognize that moral reform does indeed happen, and it happens because reformers are able to point their society to something beyond what they already believe: something that transcends the group, something that is objective to the group. Moral reform occurs when a group recognizes that what they believed was wrong, but if what makes something right is *simply* that the group believes it, this would be impossible. 

## Summary

So when we think carefully about moral relativism, we find that it faces a number of significant objections that make it very difficult to hold. Even if it seems to cohere with our desire to embrace cultural diversity, that doesn't mean that we should reject cultural diversity, it means that we shouldn't *confuse* that with moral diversity. Just because there are different moral codes doesn't mean that they are all equally good. 

In this video we've looked at the branch of metaethics known as descriptivism. This branch struggles with the question of the objectivity or relativity of moral codes. Is morality something we create, or is it something that we discover? And we've learned that it is indeed a difficult question to answer. While relativism seems to cohere with our general desire to avoid ethnocentrism, it's not clear that it's a logically coherent view of morality. If it turns out that morality is indeed objective in some sense, we're going to need a clearer picture of exactly what that is: what is it that makes morality objective?
